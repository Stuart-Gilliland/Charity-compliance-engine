Why Safeguarding Matters






Safeguarding simply means keeping people safe from harm. As a Strathaven Community Trust volunteer, you are in a position of trust — and that makes you an important part of how we protect the people we serve.




Strathaven Community Trust brings together people of all ages and backgrounds — families, older adults, people going through difficult times. Most people you meet will be absolutely fine. But occasionally, someone may need extra protection.


You don't need to be an expert. You just need to know what to look out for, who to tell, and how to respond calmly and helpfully if something concerns you. This guide covers all of that.




The most important thing to know


You are 
not expected to investigate
 concerns yourself. You are not expected to be certain. You are simply expected to 
notice, record, and report
 — and we will take it from there.








2


Who We Are Protecting








👦 Children & Young People


Anyone 
under 18
. Children have an absolute right to be safe from harm, and we have a legal duty to act on any concern about their welfare.






🧑 Adults at Risk


An adult who, due to disability, illness, mental health, age, or circumstance, may be 
unable to protect themselves
 from harm or exploitation.






In practice, this includes a wide range of people who use our services — you don't always know someone's full situation, so it's always better to follow the safeguarding approach with anyone you are concerned about.






3


Recognising Signs of Harm




Abuse and neglect take different forms. You won't always see obvious signs — and a single sign on its own may mean nothing. It's a pattern of concerns, or something that feels distinctly wrong, that matters most.






🤛


Physical Harm


Unexplained injuries, bruising in unusual places, flinching at sudden movements, fear of going home.






💬


Emotional Harm


Persistent low self-esteem, anxiety, withdrawal, extreme behaviour, crying or distress without clear reason.






⚠️


Sexual Harm


Age-inappropriate sexual knowledge, unexplained gifts, reluctance to be around a particular person.






🍽️


Neglect


Poor hygiene, hunger, inadequate clothing for the weather, always tired, untreated medical needs.






💸


Financial Harm


Especially for adults — someone else controlling their money, unexplained transfers, going without essentials.






🎣


Exploitation


Unexplained new adults in a young person's life, going missing, receiving gifts from unknown sources.








Trust your instincts


If something feels wrong — even if you can't put your finger on exactly what — that's enough reason to mention it to the DSL. You won't be wrong for raising a genuine concern.

6


How to Behave Safely as a Volunteer




Good safeguarding isn't just about responding to concerns — it's about how you conduct yourself every day. These simple habits protect the people you work with, and they protect you too.






Working with Children




Never be alone with a child in a private space


Keep doors open during one-to-one activity


Don't give out your personal phone number


Don't connect with young people on personal social media


Physical contact should be appropriate and limited


Use age-appropriate language at all times








Working with Adults at Risk




Respect their choices and autonomy


Don't handle their money or personal documents


Be alert to signs of controlling relationships


Never assist with financial transactions


Any personal care must be agreed and appropriate


Raise concerns if something changes suddenly










Photography & Social Media


Never photograph or film participants — children or adults — without prior written consent. Never share images on personal social media. If in doubt about any image, ask the DSL before sharing.

Confidentiality — What It Means Here




One of the most common questions volunteers ask is: "But they told me in confidence — do I still have to report it?"




























General matters


Low-level concerns


Risk of harm


Keep private


Discuss with DSL


Always report












When safety is at risk, the duty to report overrides confidentiality




When someone tells you something in confidence, you should treat it sensitively. But if what they tell you suggests they — or someone else — could be harmed, 
you must pass it on to the DSL
. This is not a breach of trust — it is your duty, and it is the law.




A simple rule to remember


You can keep general things private. You 
cannot keep harm private
. If in doubt — speak to the DSL. Even a quiet, informal conversation is better than staying silent.








9


Looking After Yourself




Dealing with a safeguarding concern — especially a disclosure — can be upsetting. It is completely normal to feel shaken, worried, or uncertain after something like this.






It's okay to feel affected


You don't need to be resilient all the time. Talk to the DSL or a trusted colleague about how you are feeling after a difficult situation.






You won't be left to deal with it alone


Once you've reported a concern, the DSL takes responsibility. It's not your burden to carry. We will support you through the process.






If you are struggling after an incident, please speak to the DSL or any member of the Board. We take volunteer wellbeing seriously and want to make sure you feel supported.






You don't need to be certain. You don't need to investigate.
You just need to 
notice, record, and report.
The rest is not your job alone.
