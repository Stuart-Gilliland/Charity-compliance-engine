Strathaven Community Trust brings together people of all ages and backgrounds — families, older adults, people going through difficult times. Most people you meet will be absolutely fine. But occasionally, someone may need extra protection.

You don't need to be an expert. You just need to know what to look out for, who to tell, and how to respond calmly and helpfully if something concerns you. This guide covers all of that.

In practice, this includes a wide range of people who use our services. When in doubt, always follow the safeguarding approach.

Abuse and neglect take different forms. You won't always see obvious signs — a single sign on its own may mean nothing. It's a pattern of concerns, or something that feels distinctly wrong, that matters most.

Follow these steps whenever you have a safeguarding concern

A disclosure is when someone tells you directly about abuse or harm — either happening to them, or to someone else. It takes courage to do this. Your response in that moment matters enormously.

Your five steps when someone discloses harm to you

Good safeguarding is not just about responding to concerns — it's about how you conduct yourself every day. These habits protect the people you work with, and they protect you too.

One of the most common questions volunteers ask is: "But they told me in confidence — do I still have to report it?"

When safety is at risk, the duty to report overrides confidentiality

When someone tells you something in confidence, you should treat it sensitively. But if what they tell you suggests they — or someone else — could be harmed, you must pass it on to the DSL. This is not a breach of trust — it is your duty, and it is the law.

Dealing with a safeguarding concern — especially a disclosure — can be upsetting. It is completely normal to feel shaken, worried, or uncertain after something like this.

If you are struggling after an incident, please speak to the DSL or any member of the Board. We take volunteer wellbeing seriously.
